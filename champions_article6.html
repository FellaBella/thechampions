Internet Use by Secondary School Students: A Digital Divide in Sustainable Societies


In many countries, secondary school students depend heavily on the internet for research, collaboration, and communication. However, patterns of internet use reveal significant inequalities that raise concerns about sustainability and fairness in education.

Students with strong digital access use the internet to explore advanced academic resources, participate in online forums, and engage in creative projects. They develop problem-solving skills and digital literacy that prepare them for higher education and global careers. In contrast, students with limited or unstable internet connections often use the web less frequently or only for basic tasks. Their opportunities to participate in interactive learning or online enrichment activities are restricted. This imbalance contributes to unequal skill development among young people. A sustainable society depends on equal access to information and learning opportunities. If large groups of students are excluded from meaningful internet use, social and economic inequalities may deepen over time.

Ensuring equal and responsible internet access for secondary school students is essential for building inclusive and sustainable communities. Bridging this divide strengthens not only individual learners but society as a whole.
